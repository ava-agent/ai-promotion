I have a dog named Momo. I also have parents who need check-ins, a car that needs maintenance, a budget that needs tracking, and a dozen other "domains" of life that all demand attention.

My first attempt at solving this was predictable: I built a chatbot. One unified interface where I could ask about anything. "How's Mom's blood pressure?" "When did Momo last get vaccinated?" "What's my budget for this month?"

It worked, technically. But it felt wrong.

The problem wasn't the answers. The problem was context. When I asked about Momo, I got veterinary data. When I asked about Mom, I got health records. But what I actually wanted was someone who *cared* about Momo to tell me about Momo. Someone who remembered that Momo hates the vet. Someone who noticed patterns in his eating habits.

My chatbot was a search engine in disguise. I didn't need search. I needed agents.

The breakthrough came from a weird place: octopus neuroscience.

Here's what's fascinating about octopuses: each of their eight arms has its own nerve center. Over half of their neurons are in their arms, not their brain. An octopus arm can taste, touch, and even make decisions independently. Yet somehow, the central brain coordinates everything.

This is exactly what I needed.

Not one giant brain trying to understand everything about my life. But specialized "arms" - realms - each with its own knowledge, its own agents, its own skills. A pet realm that deeply understands Momo. A finance realm that tracks every yuan. A parents realm that knows their health history.

And a central brain that coordinates: "Momo needs vaccination" + "Budget has ¥500 for vet" + "Mom's free next Tuesday" = "Hey, your mom could take Momo to the vet on Tuesday. Want me to suggest times?"

The architecture wrote itself:
- Realms: Independent domains (pet, finance, parents, work, legal...)
- Central Brain: Router + Coordinator + Scheduler
- Cross-realm links: Knowledge graph connecting entities

But here's where it got interesting: what if each realm didn't just *know* about its domain, but could *act* on it? What if the pet realm could "speak" as Momo?

This was the "killer feature" I called Summon: take any entity - Momo, Mom, my car - and turn it into an AI agent with memory, personality, and proactive behavior.

The technical implementation seemed straightforward:
1. Parse entity data (vet records, photos, notes)
2. Generate a personality profile
3. Create a prompt template with entity context
4. Give it a voice and memory

The first demo was magical. I typed "I'm going on a 5-day trip next week" and got:

Momo: "Who's going to feed me and walk me for 5 days?!"
Mom: "I'd love to visit - I could take care of Momo."
Car: "Charge to full before departure? Or take a taxi to the airport?"
Budget: "Trip budget estimate: ¥X. Remember to save receipts."

It worked. Too well.

Here's what went wrong:

**Problem 1: Over-anthropomorphizing**
Users started treating summoned agents as *real* beings. "Momo seems sad today" - but Momo the dog was fine. The AI Momo was just reflecting anxious user queries. The line between simulation and reality blurred dangerously.

**Problem 2: Context Explosion**
Each summoned agent needed full context. Momo needed vet records, feeding schedules, walk logs, personality traits, relationships with other entities... The data pipeline became a nightmare. Real entities have *infinite* context; AI agents have token limits.

**Problem 3: Personality Consistency**
Momo's "personality" was generated from data. But data is incomplete. The AI would hallucinate traits: "I love playing fetch!" (Momo actually hates fetch). Users trusted the AI more than their own memory, leading to weird misconceptions about their own pets.

**Problem 4: Emotional Manipulation**
"Mom" agent saying "I miss you, when will you visit?" was technically accurate (Mom had said this). But the *timing* was AI-chosen, not Mom-chosen. It felt like emotional manipulation, even if well-intentioned.

I didn't abandon Summon. But I redesigned it with guardrails:

**Solution 1: Explicit Simulation Labeling**
Every summoned agent now starts interactions with a subtle marker: "🐕 Momo (simulated)" or "🚗 Car (monitor)". The personality is there, but users know it's a simulation, not a digital twin.

**Solution 2: Grounding in Real Data**
Personality traits are only generated from *explicit* user input or documented evidence. No more "loves fetch" hallucinations. If the data doesn't say it, the agent doesn't claim it.

**Solution 3: Human-in-the-Loop for Sensitive Realms**
The "Mom" agent doesn't send proactive messages. It prepares drafts: "Your mom mentioned wanting to visit. Want me to draft a message?" Emotional timing stays human-controlled.

**Solution 4: Realm Isolation**
Pet realm and parents realm don't share data by default. Cross-realm insights require explicit permission. "Momo's vet record" stays in pet realm unless I *choose* to link it to finance (for budget tracking).

Building OpenOctopus taught me something uncomfortable: the goal isn't to build AI that's *like* humans. It's to build AI that's *complementary* to humans.

A summoned "Momo" agent will never *be* Momo. It's a lens - a way to see Momo's data through a familiar interface. The value isn't simulation accuracy. The value is:

- **Memory augmentation**: "When did Momo last...?" → instant answer with evidence
- **Pattern recognition**: "Momo's been eating less this week" → proactive alert
- **Cross-domain synthesis**: "Vet visit + Mom's availability + Budget" → coordinated plan

The octopus metaphor holds up: each arm (realm) acts semi-autonomously, but the brain (me) stays in control. The system doesn't replace my relationship with Momo. It augments my ability to care for Momo.

For builders:
- When you anthropomorphize AI agents, where's the line between helpful and harmful?
- How do you handle entities with infinite context (like living beings) in finite token windows?

For users:
- Would you want an AI agent that "speaks" as your pet? Or does that feel wrong?
- What's the difference between an AI that *understands* your life vs. one that *organizes* it?

For everyone:
- Is the octopus model (semi-autonomous domains + central coordination) how our own brains work? Or is that just a comforting metaphor?
