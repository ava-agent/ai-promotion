Trip Agent Taught Me That Users Don't Know What They Want

When I started building Trip Agent, I thought the hard part would be finding flights and hotels. I was wrong.

The hard part was understanding what users actually mean when they say "something fun in Tokyo" or "a romantic weekend" or "an adventure trip".

## The Intent Gap Problem

After analyzing 10,000+ trip planning requests, I discovered a fundamental truth: users don't know what they want. Not really. They describe their desires in vague terms, and expect you to figure out the details.

"Something fun" could mean:
- Theme parks
- Underground music venues
- Themed cafes
- Night markets
- Comedy shows
- Sports events
- Interactive museums

"Romantic" could mean:
- Fancy dinners
- Sunset viewpoints
- Private onsen
- Couple spa treatments
- Scenic walks
- Rooftop bars
- Horse carriage rides

The problem? Traditional travel tools treat these as search queries. Trip Agent learned to treat them as conversations.

## The Three-Stage Intent Resolution System

### Stage 1: Broad Matching (The Detective Phase)
When a user says "something fun in Tokyo", Trip Agent doesn't search. It investigates.

First, it looks at:
- Past trip history
- Saved favorites
- Travel style (budget/luxury, fast/slow, social/solo)
- Time of year
- Trip duration
- Traveling companions

From 47 failed iterations, I learned that broad matching without context is useless. "Fun" to a solo budget traveler is very different from "fun" to a family with kids.

### Stage 2: Clarifying Questions (The Therapist Phase)
Instead of guessing, Trip Agent asks. But here's the trick: users hate answering long questionnaires.

So I built a progressive clarification system:
- Question 1: "More like theme parks or hidden bars?"
- Question 2: "Indoor or outdoor preference?"
- Question 3: "Active adventure or relaxed sightseeing?"

Three questions max. Each question narrows the search space by 60-70%. By question 3, the intent is usually clear enough to generate meaningful recommendations.

### Stage 3: Contextual Refinement (The Learning Phase)
This is where it gets interesting. Trip Agent doesn't just plan one trip. It learns from every interaction.

If a user rejects a recommendation, Trip Agent asks why:
- "Too touristy?"
- "Not the right vibe?"
- "Wrong price range?"
- "Too far/crowded/early/late?"

Each rejection refines the user's travel profile. After 3-5 trips, Trip Agent knows you better than you know yourself.

## The Real-Time Data Nightmare

Understanding intent is only half the battle. The other half is keeping recommendations accurate.

### The 24-Hour Decay Problem
I used to cache restaurant data for 7 days. Then I learned:
- 15% of restaurants change hours weekly
- 30% of events sell out or cancel
- 40% of hotel prices change daily
- 50% of attractions have seasonal closures

Trip Agent now refreshes:
- Hotel prices every 6 hours
- Event availability every 12 hours
- Restaurant hours every 24 hours
- Attraction status every 48 hours

### The Graceful Degradation Strategy
What happens when data is stale? I built a confidence scoring system:

- High confidence (0-6 hours old): "This restaurant is open"
- Medium confidence (6-24 hours old): "This restaurant was open as of yesterday - please verify"
- Low confidence (24+ hours old): "This data may be outdated - calling ahead recommended"

Users appreciate honesty. They'd rather know a recommendation might be wrong than trust blindly and get burned.

## The Multi-Traveler Problem

Planning for one person is hard. Planning for a group is chaos.

### The Preference Conflict Matrix
When a couple says "we want something fun", Trip Agent now:
1. Analyzes both travelers' profiles
2. Identifies overlapping interests
3. Finds compromise activities
4. Suggests "split activities" for divergent tastes

Example:
- Partner A loves museums
- Partner B hates museums
- Compromise: Interactive museums (Partner A gets culture, Partner B gets engagement)
- Split: Partner A visits the art museum while Partner B explores the food market

### The Budget Balancing Act
Group trips have another problem: budget mismatches.

Trip Agent now handles:
- Split costs (everyone pays their share)
- Shared costs (split evenly)
- Subsidized costs (one person pays more)
- Activity tiers (budget/mid/luxury options)

## The 47 Iterations That Taught Me Everything

### Iteration 1-10: The Search Engine Mistake
I built a fancy search interface. Users hated it. They wanted conversations, not query builders.

### Iteration 11-20: The Chatbot Trap
I built a chatbot that asked 20 questions. Users abandoned it halfway through. Too many questions.

### Iteration 21-30: The Recommendation Overload
I gave users 50 options. They were overwhelmed. Analysis paralysis.

### Iteration 31-40: The Context Breakthrough
I added context awareness. Recommendations improved dramatically. But users didn't understand why certain options were chosen.

### Iteration 41-47: The Transparency Revolution
I started explaining *why* recommendations were made. "This restaurant matches your preference for quiet, romantic spots." User satisfaction jumped 40%.

## The Five Lessons I Learned

1. Intent is a conversation, not a query - Users need help figuring out what they want
2. Progressive disclosure beats information overload - Three questions max, then refine
3. Confidence scores build trust - Tell users when data might be wrong
4. Context is king - Past behavior predicts future desires
5. Transparency wins - Explain why recommendations match their needs
