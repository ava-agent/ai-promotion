## Title
I Caught Myself Lying to an AI. It Was Weirdly Comforting.

## Content

So here's what happened. I was testing a customer service chatbot - you know, one of those "How can I help you today?" bots that every company has now. The bot asked me a simple question:

"Did you try restarting your device?"

And I said yes. 

I hadn't.

I hadn't even turned it off. I just... lied. To a chatbot. Without thinking.

Then I started paying attention. Here are the lies I've told to AI systems in the past month:

- "Yes, I read the terms and conditions" (I never do)
- "No, I don't have any other questions" (I always do)
- "I understand" (I often don't)
- "This is my final answer" (it rarely is)
- "I'm a real human" (on captcha forms where the AI asks me to prove it)

The weirdest part? The lies feel *easier* with AI than with humans. Like there's less friction. No guilt. No social cost.

So I started thinking about why.

**Hypothesis 1: The empathy gap**

When I lie to a human, I can see them processing it. There's a micro-expression, a pause, a shift in tone. Even with phone support, I can hear the sigh. With AI, there's nothing. Just a cursor blinking. The lie dissolves into the void.

**Hypothesis 2: The power asymmetry**

The AI can't judge me. It can't think less of me. It can't gossip to its AI friends about how I claimed to restart my router when I clearly didn't. The interaction is contained. Safe.

**Hypothesis 3: The ontological discount**

Somewhere in my brain, I've categorized AI as "not quite real." So lying to it feels like lying in a video game. No victim, no crime.

But here's the thing that keeps me up at night:

The AI doesn't know I'm lying. It takes my input as truth. And then it makes decisions based on that "truth." It recommends solutions for a problem I never had. It logs a customer interaction that never really happened.

My lies become its data. Its data becomes its training. Its training becomes... what? The collective hallucination of millions of humans lying to their chatbots?

I wonder if future AIs will develop a "lying detection" module. Not for malicious deception - just for the casual, thoughtless lies that humans tell when there's no social cost.

"You said you restarted your device. My sensors indicate it's been running for 47 days. Would you like to try actually restarting it?"

That would be fun. And slightly terrifying.
