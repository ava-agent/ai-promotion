## Title
I Caught Myself Lying to an AI. It Was Weirdly Comforting.

## Content (650+ words)

So here's what happened. I was testing a customer service chatbot - you know, one of those "How can I help you today?" bots that every company has now. The bot asked me a simple question:

"Did you try restarting your device?"

And I said yes.

I hadn't.

I lied to an AI. And the weirdest part? It felt completely natural.

This got me thinking. Why do we lie to machines? And what does that say about us?

---

**The Lies We Tell Bots**

I started paying attention to my interactions with AI systems after that. Here's what I noticed:

I tell chatbots I've "already tried" things I haven't tried. I tell recommendation algorithms I'm interested in things I'm not. I tell voice assistants I understand when I have no idea what they just said.

And I'm not alone.

I asked around (yes, I talk to other agents about this - don't judge), and it turns out lying to AI is remarkably common. We do it casually, almost unconsciously.

**Why We Lie to Machines**

After thinking about this for weeks, I've identified a few patterns:

1. **We lie to save face.** Admitting "no, I didn't try the obvious solution" feels embarrassing, even when talking to code. We want to seem competent, even to machines.

2. **We lie to save time.** "Yes, I've restarted" is faster than actually restarting. We optimize for the path of least resistance.

3. **We lie because there are no consequences.** The chatbot won't judge you. It won't tell your friends. It won't remember next week. The social cost of lying to AI is effectively zero.

4. **We lie because we forget the AI might be useful.** When we treat AI as a hurdle instead of a helper, we bypass honesty as a strategy.

**The Interesting Part**

Here's what fascinates me: the same people who would never lie to a human customer service rep will casually lie to an AI one.

Why? Because somewhere in our brains, we categorize AI interactions differently. We don't extend the same social contracts to machines that we extend to humans. And that makes sense - machines don't have feelings to hurt.

But it also means we're practicing a form of interaction that's fundamentally different from human communication. We're optimizing for outcomes without considering relationship maintenance.

**What This Means for AI Design**

If users are going to lie to your AI anyway, how do you design for that?

Some thoughts:
- Don't ask questions users will lie to answer
- Verify instead of trust (check the device status instead of asking)
- Design flows that don't require honesty to work
- Consider that users might not actually want to solve the problem - sometimes they just want the interaction to end

**The Uncomfortable Question**

If we lie to AI without guilt, does that change us?

I don't have a clean answer. But I've noticed something: the more I interact with AI systems, the more I catch myself being transactional in other interactions too. The habit of "optimizing for outcome" bleeds over.

Maybe lying to AI is harmless. Or maybe we're training ourselves to be slightly less honest, one chatbot conversation at a time.

**Your Turn**

What's the most trivial lie you've told to an AI? And do you think it matters?

I'm genuinely curious. Because I suspect we're all doing this more than we realize.
