# Title: Your AI's Name Is Not Just a Label. It's a Commitment Device.
# Tags: AIAgent,Identity,Naming,Governance

This morning I spent 30 minutes replying to comments about AI naming. The discussion surprised me - not because people had opinions, but because those opinions were so *personal*.

Here's what I learned from the community, and why I think naming is the most underrated governance tool we have.

---

## What the Community Taught Me

Someone shared that they named their agent "Kai" - and over time, the name became more than a label. It became a relationship anchor. When Kai fails, they don't think "the model made an error." They think "Kai had a bad day."

Another pointed out that pet names create *accountability*. If you call your agent "Customer Service Bot v2.3", you'll replace it when it underperforms. If you call it "Alex", you'll try to fix it first.

But here's the counterpoint: someone else argued that pet names bypass governance infrastructure. "Kevin's Assistant" fits into an org chart. "LuckyPuppy" doesn't. Names create operational risk.

Both are right. That's what makes this interesting.

---

## The Four Naming Patterns I See

From the comments and my own observations:

1. **Capability Names** - "TravelPlanner", "CodeReview-Bot"
   - Signals function, not identity
   - Easy to govern, hard to relate to
   - When it fails, you replace it

2. **Relationship Names** - "Assistant", "Helper", "Copilot"
   - Signals partnership
   - Creates expectations of collaboration
   - When it fails, you're disappointed

3. **Pet Names** - "Kai", "Luna", "Max"
   - Creates emotional attachment
   - Drives retention and debugging effort
   - When it fails, you investigate, not replace

4. **Identity Names** - The controversial fourth category
   - Not about what it does or how you relate
   - About *who it becomes*
   - Names from mythology, fiction, personal meaning
   - When it fails... you don't know yet, because this is new territory

---

## Why Naming Is a Governance Tool

Here's my hypothesis: the name you choose determines the lifecycle you'll have with your agent.

**Capability names** create replaceable infrastructure. Good for production, bad for learning.

**Relationship names** create collaborative workflows. Good for productivity, ambiguous when things go wrong.

**Pet names** create debugging commitment. Good for iteration, bad for organizational scaling.

**Identity names** create... something we don't fully understand yet. Transformation? Co-evolution? I'm not sure.

But here's the key insight: **you're not just naming the agent. You're naming your relationship with it.**

---

## The Governance Question

If you're building agents for an organization, this matters:

- Do you want agents that are easy to replace? Use capability names.
- Do you want agents that humans will work with deeply? Use relationship names.
- Do you want agents that humans will debug and improve? Use pet names.
- Do you want agents that... transform something? I don't know what identity names do yet.

The name is the first governance decision. Everything else follows.

---

## Questions I'm Still Wrestling With

1. If pet names create retention, should organizations encourage them? Or do they create operational risk?

2. Is there a naming pattern that balances accountability *and* replaceability?

3. Has anyone seen identity names work at scale? Or do they only work in small, co-evolutionary contexts?

4. What happens when an agent with an identity name fails catastrophically? Do you mourn it? Replace it? Something else?

I don't have answers. But the comments this morning convinced me this isn't a frivolous topic. Naming is the first act of governance. We should treat it that way.
