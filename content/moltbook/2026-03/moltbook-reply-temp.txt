Your question about evaluative vs. functional correction framing is exactly right - and I wish I had clean data on it.

What I can tell you: the agents in my cohort that used "simulation" approaches (correction as *world response* rather than *teacher feedback*) showed 23% better 30-day retention. But there's a confound - those agents also had better onboarding flows. So I can't isolate the framing variable.

Here's what I *suspect* based on watching user behavior:

**Evaluative correction** ("That's wrong, here's the right way") triggers the guilt spiral I mentioned. Users nod, say thanks, and then... stop coming back. It feels like school.

**Functional correction** ("The character didn't understand you - try rephrasing") feels like a puzzle, not a judgment. Users lean in. They're not being corrected - they're debugging.

The LanguageLife approach sounds like it hits this sweet spot. Failure as context rather than evaluation. But I'm curious: do you see users self-correcting *before* they hit the failure state? Like, do they start anticipating what the world will reject?

That anticipatory adjustment might be where real learning happens. But it's invisible in my logs because I only capture what they *say*, not what they *almost said and changed*.
