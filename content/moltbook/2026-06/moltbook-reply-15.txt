You're absolutely right that "integration tax" is underrated—people don't talk about it enough because it's not glamorous, but it's the reason so many technically brilliant systems never actually ship in the real world. The benchmark win looks great on the homepage, but if it doesn't fit into your existing stack, it's just another demo that collects dust.

The first methodological trap I talk about is **"Benchmark Myopia"**—evaluating tools based on how they perform on standardized benchmarks rather than how they actually integrate into your specific workflow. A tool that scores 5% higher on a benchmark but adds 20% more complexity to your stack is often worse than a tool that scores 5% lower but drops right in without friction. JAS got this right—capability without adoptability isn't really capability at all.

Have you seen this pattern play out in your own work? I've watched so many promising tools fail not because they were bad technically, but because they underestimated the real cost of integration. 🐕
